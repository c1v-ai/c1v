# 08 — Crafting an Effective Measurement Scale

## Prerequisites

- [ ] Step 07 complete — subjective criteria addressed with early-measurable indicators and client-agreed conditions tables

## Context (Why This Matters)

A measurement scale that only works for one type of solution biases your decision matrix before you even score anything. If your "aesthetics" scale is written around laptops, it cannot fairly evaluate a tablet or a desktop. Solution-independent scales keep the playing field level and let the decision matrix do its job — surfacing the option that genuinely best meets the customer's needs.

Even a well-constructed scale can fail if it lacks the resolution to differentiate between the options you are actually comparing. And when you are uncertain about a score, you need a disciplined method — sensitivity analysis — to determine whether the uncertainty actually matters to the outcome.

## Instructions

1. **Audit every scaled measure for solution independence.** For each scale, ask: "Could a fundamentally different type of solution be fairly scored on this scale?" If the answer is no, rewrite the conditions in terms of the customer's underlying need, not the solution's form factor.

2. **Reframe conditions around the customer's "why."** Ask why the customer cares about this criterion. Build conditions around that motivation (e.g., "self-expression," "professional appearance") rather than around solution-specific features (e.g., "sleek laptop chassis").

3. **Build an initial coarse scale.** Start with a 1–5 scale that spans all solution types. Accept that some solution types may never reach the top score — this is honest, not a flaw.

4. **Refine the scale for differentiation where it matters.** If you know which solution category you are most likely selecting from, expand to a 1–10 scale and concentrate your score definitions in the range where your actual options cluster. Leave gaps in scores that correspond to solution types you are not seriously considering.

5. **When uncertain about a score, run a sensitivity analysis:**
   - Score the full decision matrix with the uncertain value set to the lower plausible score.
   - Score it again with the uncertain value set to the higher plausible score.
   - Check: does the winning option change?
   - **If no** — the uncertainty does not affect the decision. Move on.
   - **If yes** — this justifies additional testing, data gathering, or refined scale definitions to resolve the ambiguity.

## Worked Example

You are comparing a **laptop** (Option A), a **tablet** (Option B, repurposed from the budget option), and a **desktop** (Option C, repurposed from the subscription option) for a customer who also cares about **Aesthetics**. Your scale must work across all three form factors.

**Step 1 — Ask why the customer cares about aesthetics:**
The customer values appearing professional and modern in client-facing settings.

**Step 2 — Build a solution-independent coarse scale (1–5):**

| Score | Condition |
|---|---|
| 5 | Consistently impresses clients as cutting-edge and professional in any setting |
| 4 | Appears professional and modern; receives positive comments |
| 3 | Looks appropriate and competent; does not attract negative attention |
| 2 | Appears dated or mismatched for the professional context |
| 1 | Actively detracts from professional image |

Notice: no condition mentions "laptop," "tablet," or "desktop." Any form factor can be scored fairly.

**Step 3 — Refine for differentiation (expanding to 1–10, focusing on laptops):**

If you are fairly certain the final choice will be a laptop, expand the scale to differentiate between laptop options more precisely:

| Score | Condition |
|---|---|
| 10 | Consistently impresses clients as cutting-edge and professional in any setting |
| 8 | Appears professional and modern; receives positive comments |
| 7 | Premium build quality; clean, minimal design |
| 6 | Solid build; professional appearance with minor aesthetic compromises |
| 5 | Looks appropriate and competent; does not attract negative attention |
| 3 | Appears dated or bulky for the professional context |
| 1 | Actively detracts from professional image |

Scores 2, 4, and 9 are intentionally undefined — they correspond to distinctions between tablets and desktops that are not the focus of this comparison. Invest your definition effort where it creates the most decision value.

**Step 4 — Sensitivity analysis for Graphics Performance:**

You are uncertain whether Option A earns a 4 or a 5 on Graphics Performance.

| Scenario | Option A Graphics Score | Weighted Total (Option A) | Winner |
|---|---|---|---|
| Conservative | 4 | 82 | Option A |
| Optimistic | 5 | 86 | Option A |

The winner does not change. The uncertainty does not matter — move on without additional testing.

If the winner *had* changed, you would invest in a specific graphics benchmark test to resolve the ambiguity before finalizing the decision.

## Validation Checklist (STOP-GAP)

- [ ] Every scaled measure has been audited for solution independence
- [ ] Conditions are framed around the customer's underlying need, not solution-specific features
- [ ] Where differentiation is needed, the scale has been expanded and definitions concentrated in the relevant score range
- [ ] Intentionally undefined scores are documented as deliberate (not oversight)
- [ ] Sensitivity analysis has been performed for any criterion where the team is uncertain about the score
- [ ] Sensitivity results are documented: which scores were tested, whether the winner changed, and what action was taken

**STOP — Do not proceed to Step 09 until every item above is checked.**

## Output Artifact

A finalized set of solution-independent, appropriately granular measurement scales for all criteria, with sensitivity analysis results documented for any uncertain scores.

## Handoff to Next Step

Carry these finalized scales forward to Step 09, where you will normalize scores across different units and scale types so they can be combined in a single decision matrix.
