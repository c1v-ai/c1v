## Course Description

One of the most challenging aspects of system engineering is guring out how best to manage system performance trade-offs. Time, budget, and other limiting resources force you to prioritize aspects of your design so your system provides the maximum benet. You can manage these trade-offs efciently and effectively by implementing a process known as Quality Function Deployment method, or QFD.

QFD is also sometimes referred to as "house of quality," and the document that is created during QFD does in fact look like a house. The house of quality provides a crucial visual link between your emerging system design, the needs of your customers, and competing designs in the marketplace. With QFD you'll build intuition about your system, make decisions about resource allocation, communicate development priorities, and establish design targets, all in a way that ensures you are creating the best-performing solution possible. Design teams of all types use QFD, and it's one of the most established and recognized systems design tools today.

In this course, you'll be led through a detailed, step-by-step process to build a house of quality for your own project. This process begins with identifying what your system must do for customers (performance measures) and which of these performance measures matter most to the customer. You'll relate each of your system's engineering characteristics to those performance measures. After this, you'll complete the house of quality by considering each engineering characteristic in terms of importance, difculty, associated cost, the current competitive benchmarks, and minimum or maximum requirement thresholds. This process will lead you to performance targets that consider both what is desired and what is feasible. The collection of design targets will give you a good working estimate of the best performance you can expect your system to achieve.

## Module Introduction: Relate System Performance to Design

![Image](/Users/davidancor/Documents/MBA/System Design - eCornell/5 - Implementing the Quality Function Deployment Method/5-HoQ_for_software_sys_design/full-instructions_artifacts/image_000008_f0c1b37060de426eb5bc80476faaed0deb9ed62443a78c66f5592a098b71f5cd.png)

The Quality Functional Design (QFD) begins with the customer needs, and these needs are expressed using a list of attributes that will be used to measure system performance. Basically, for each of the important criteria (reliability, accuracy, portability, safety, etc.) you're using a performance measure to express how well your system scores. In addition to a scaled score for performance, each of the criteria are weighted for importance relative to the other criteria. This lets you focus on what matters most to customers while still accounting for all important criteria.

With broad performance measures established, you then need to turn your attention to the actual nuts and bolts of your system. For example, how might your system's mass affect criteria like portability and safety? By focusing on specics, you can connect the detailed characteristics of your system to a measurement of how well it will meet customer's needs. This is a key outcome from using the QFD method, and making this connection will be the focus of your work in this module.

In this module, you'll begin to build your house of quality. You'll start by establishing performance criteria and determining their relative importance with respect to customer needs. You'll use these criteria to evaluate your system's performance against competitors' performance. Next you'll identify all engineering characteristics of your system and assess their impact on the performance criteria. Finally, you'll examine interrelationships between engineering characteristics so you can better understand how tradeoffs might impact your design.

The QFD method brings into focus the interplay between system performance, customer needs, and specic design targets.

A good approach to setting design targets takes into account both the impact on performance measures and the potential impact on other design targets.

- possible result that one or more subsystems will not perform as well?
- Do we require a low error rate, or increase the allowable error rate and make our system handle errors more easily?

Throughout the design process you need to address these kinds of trade-offs in a way that results in the best system-and in a way that you can justify to others. The design decisions you make along the way will result in specic design targets. These design targets are desired values that you set for each of your system's engineering characteristics, such as how fast your system can go or how much it weighs. But how do you know that the design targets you've determined are appropriate?

In order to show that your design targets will result in a system that meets customer's needs, it is important that you:

- dene performance criteria for your system
- relate each of the performance criteria to customer needs
- establish the relationship between the performance criteria and each engineering characteristic
- understand how adjustments to design targets for each engineering characteristic will inuence performance measures; and
- understand how adjusting one design target may also inuence the ease of meeting other design targets

Since your QFD will be used as a communication tool throughout the project and across your team, it will be useful to include additional descriptions for some of the performance criteria. This additional information can be provided as an optional extension of the QFD.

## Module Wrap-up: Define and Refine Design Targets

In this module, you determined initial targets for engineering characteristics. You also calculated performance estimates for a system based on your initial design targets. In this way, you have forged a connection between the engineering characteristics for your initial design and the performance measures your customers care about.

Now with a QFD that expresses relationships between engineering characteristics and other engineering characteristics as well as these characteristics' impact on performance, you can communicate the relationships and examine trade-offs at the level of the individual characteristic. And you can argue effectively that your chosen design targets will lead your team to a winning solution.
