# Crawley Ch 4 — Form

Crawley Ch 4 defines form as what the system IS — the physical or informational embodiment that exists (or could exist) and is instrumental in executing function. Every form passes two tests: it exists, and it carries function.

## Form decomposes into objects + structure

- **Objects** — the elements of form, the parts/modules/routines.
- **Structure** — the set of formal relationships between those objects. "The set of relationships that exist and do not require change, exchange, or anything that happens along a timeline" (line 1619).

Box 4.1 establishes form as the static, perseverant side of the system — what remains when the system is not operating.

## Three types of formal relationships (§4.4, lines 1810–1817)

- **Spatial/topological** — location, placement (above/below/within/adjacent/aligned). Matters because it may enable "ballistic" interactions or inform performance.
- **Connectivity** — what is connected to what. The enabler of most functional interactions, because transfers require carriers: wires carry current, arteries carry blood, shared addresses carry data.
- **Intangible** — address, sequence, membership, ownership, human bonds. Static but changeable.

## Representation: OPM graphs and DSMs

Form is represented two ways, always equivalently:
- **OPM graphs** — objects as boxes, relationships as labeled single-headed arrows (direction arbitrary; label describes relationship).
- **Design Structure Matrices (DSM)** — N×N, read-down-the-column-to-the-row (Box 4.6, line 1704). Symmetric for structure because structure has no causality.

## Identification procedure (line 1639)

"Examine each pairwise set of objects and ask: is this spatial/topological (or connectivity) relationship key to some important functional interaction or to the successful emergence of function and performance?"

This is the filter that prevents over-specification.

## Three-layer context model (§4.5, lines 1820–1865)

- **Product/system** — what the architect owns.
- **Whole product system** — product + accompanying systems necessary for value (the pump plus hoses, mount, power, operator).
- **Use context** — what is present during operation but optional (physical room, climate, nearby infrastructure).

**Architects are "accountable two levels out in context and two levels down in decomposition."**

## Software IS form (§4.6, lines 1866–1977)

**Principle of Dualism (Box 4.7, line 1888):** all built systems exist simultaneously in physical and informational domains. Informational form must always be encoded in physical form (poems in print; thoughts in neural patterns; DVDs in optical markings).

Code IS form — it exists prior to execution, is instrumental when interpreted as instructions. Software's:
- **Spatial/topological structure** = sequence + containment (which lines are inside which loops — think indentation).
- **Connectivity structure** = shared variables.

Every architectural concept in Ch 4 applies to software unchanged.
