Exploring Your System's Architecture
FFBD Overview
Key Points
Defining a systems’s architecture is considered a discovery process, so expect to iterate and then iterate some more.

The FFBD, in some variation, has been used for over a century because of its ease of use and effectiveness. It is important to know which variation your team agrees upon to use.

Unless your system is remarkably simple (like "alarm clock" simple), defining a system's architecture is considered a discovery process. As you strive to describe your system you begin to learn more about its intricacies, which, in turn, may inspire you to change your system for the better. Rarely are our first ideas the best. By exploring them, we can almost always discover new, important aspects and needs that must be addressed, leading to better ways to develop our solution.

The FFBD has been used in some form for possibly over a hundred years, and the fact that it's still used today speaks to how useful it can be. Because it has been around for so long, there are several variations that exist. Each variation has slightly different formatting rules and symbolic representations. In this course we will describe a variation that adjusts easily to many other variations, and provide some common practices from other variations that you may find helpful. The most important thing, though, is that whatever variation you use, your team is consistent in the way they use it to help ensure it is properly interpreted by all.

Other system architecture tools, such as Activity Diagrams or IDEF0 Diagrams, can meet many of the same design needs as an FFBD. However, the flexibility and ease of use of the FFBD and its variations continues to make it a great tool that is used widely in many professional circles.

In this course we'll be using Microsoft PowerPoint in our example, and we'll ask that you also use PowerPoint in your activities and course project work. We chose this tool primarily because of its nearly universal availability and its presumed familiarity to most users. If you’re familiar with an Activity Diagram or an IDEF0 Diagram, you should probably be able to transition fairly easily to using either of these in place of an FFBD after completing the course. Likewise, you may wish to explore other software tools, like Visio and CORE, that can also be used effectively to create FFBD diagrams. Regardless of what tool you use, you should expect to go through several iterations of your definition document.


## Creating an Initial FFBD
Key Points
Often an initial, concise FFBD helps capture the overall operation of the system. Later, details are added to expose more of the system functionality.

Even a simple FFBD can serve as a catalyst for team discussion and can improve awareness of how system functions interact.

The FFBD is a fairly straightforward tool. Let's walk through an example of how an FFBD is used to describe a system. We'll define the components of the FFBD more formally throughout the course, but for now let's look at how the process of creating an FFBD might begin.

The FFBD example we'll look at was created to describe the system for a health monitoring wearable device. First let's look at how a team might have described the project when they got started:

The wrist band communicates wirelessly to the chest strap, and both should have their own battery and microcontroller to operate the sensors.
The sensors monitor a variety of health statistics, including blood pressure, heart rate, blood dissolved oxygen, and blood flow rate.
Each sensor also has its own special requirements. For example, some need the wrist band and chest straps sensors to work together and others can potentially cause interference for one another.
The system provides regular updates to the user, but requires some information from the user regarding their current activity (e.g., user is at rest; user is exercising; user is doing something stressful). If the system detects a potential issue, it notifies the user. Then depending upon the potential severity, it alerts people in the local vicinity, as well as emergency medical professionals.
Now let's see how some of this might translate to an FFBD. For the moment, we're only going to focus on a small part of the functionality described. simple FFBD
According to the FFBD, the first function to perform in the system's operation is to securely attach the device (F.1.1). To someone like the person working on the sensors, this functionality may seem implicit. However, to the person working on the mechanical housing and ergonomic use of the system, this is a very critical function that needs to be highlighted.

Similarly, the mechanical housing designer may think the next function of turning on the device (F.1.2) as being implicit. However, stating this explicitly may aid the person working on power to help others recognize that the start-up of the system can have an initial transient.

The next block, "Calibrate Device," (F.1.3) may inspire more conversation between the mechanical housing designer and the engineer in charge of sensors. Together they may recognize the need for easier, more reliable calibration.

The specific details of how to format the FFBD properly will be covered later in the course. But even this basic example demonstrates how an FFBD can help the team begin to recognize the interconnected nature of their individual work.
