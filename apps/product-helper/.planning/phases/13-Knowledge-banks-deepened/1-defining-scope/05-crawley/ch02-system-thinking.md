# Crawley Ch 2 — System Thinking

Crawley Ch 2 frames system thinking as the execution of four sequential-but-iterative tasks. A system is "a set of entities and their relationships, whose functionality is greater than the sum of the individual entities" (Box 2.1). The non-negotiable is **emergence**: "the interaction of entities leads to emergence … it is this emergence that can give systems added value" (Box 2.2).

Emergence fills a 2×2 (anticipated/unanticipated × desirable/undesirable): cars transport people (anticipated-desirable), cars create freedom (unanticipated-desirable), cars burn hydrocarbons (anticipated-undesirable), cars kill people (unanticipated-undesirable).

## Task 1 — Identify the system, its form, and its function

Form is what the system IS; function is what it DOES, and function decomposes into *process* (the transformation) and *operand* (the object whose state changes). The triple instrument-process-operand ("I pump water" → pump/pumping/water) is canonical — "either fundamental to all systems or fundamental to the way the human brain understands all systems" (line 676).

## Task 2 — Identify entities, boundary, and context

Crawley's five-step procedure:
1. Define an initial decomposition into entities (easy for discrete systems like the solar system, hard for integral systems like car steering).
2. Enumerate potential entities by **holistic thinking** (the Saarinen principle: "always design a thing by considering it in its next larger context").
3. Apply **focus** to winnow to 7±2 (Sherlock Holmes: "I see no more than you, but I have trained myself to notice what I see.").
4. Create abstractions that surface the important details and hide the rest.
5. Define the system boundary, which separates system from context and identifies the interfaces that must be controlled.

## Task 3 — Identify relationships

Identify relationships among entities, including external interfaces (boundary crossings are interfaces).

## Task 4 — Predict emergence

Anticipated+desirable is the target, but unanticipated+undesirable (the "emergency") is what the architect must also forecast.

## KB-1 operational consequence

For KB-1, these four tasks ARE the scope-definition procedure: an intake that doesn't execute all four leaves the problem under-specified and propagates ambiguity downstream to KBs 3–7.
